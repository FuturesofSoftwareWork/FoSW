AI is reshaping software companies at multiple levels. Its effects reach far beyond individual development tasks, influencing how companies create value, organize work, make decisions, develop capabilities, and compete.

For software leaders, the key question is how the organization can capture the value AI makes possible. This requires more than efficient use of GenAI tools by individual developers. It requires the ability to redesign value streams, governance, roles, capabilities, and business choices around AI’s new possibilities and constraints.

This is why AI adoption requires agility beyond software development teams. Team-level agility remains important, while successful adoption also depends on business agility, organizational agility, and enterprise agility.

Agility means the capability to sense change and respond to it effectively. In the context of AI, this means continuously understanding how AI changes software work, where it creates value, where new bottlenecks emerge, and what organizational responses are needed.

**Key Agility Definitions**

- **Software (development) agility** entails adapting to changes in requirements, technology and business environments while delivering appropriate high-quality software products fast.
- **Organizational agility** is the capability at the organizational level to sense changes and respond to them appropriately and swiftly (e.g., by reconfiguring the resources or restructuring the organization).
- **Business agility** means that the company can respond to, and possibly also create changes based on customer needs and market opportunities, for example by shaping its strategy and business models.
- **Enterprise agility** incorporates the sensing and responding capabilities at the firm (enterprise) level. It combines business and organizational agility capabilities.

All of those are are established concepts, and relevant in software companies and software development organizations.

## AI Adoption in Software Development and Organizations

In established software development organizations, software work has been conducted previously with various process models and workflow arrangements. In principle, every software organization has some actual software development processes and workflows which may or may not have been defined fully explicitly. In addition, they may occasionally vary—perhaps even unconsciously—depending on the current circumstances. Nevertheless, every time new software is created and delivered, the organization has followed some processes and workflows—at least ad hoc or accidental.

Now that various AI tools have been and are taken into use, they in practice affect those software processes and workflows in one way or another. They may change the ways of doing various software engineering tasks (currently especially coding), they may make some software engineering tasks unnecessary, and they may introduce new necessary tasks and activities, such as prompt management.

In all, it is crucial to realize how such various changes affect the software work across the organization vertically and horizontally—all the way from the product development inception to the customer/user delivery and evolution. Where are business and customer value created and how do new AI technology adoptions influence value creation? For instance, if software developers with AI assistant tools can produce software code faster than before, what is the net-effect in terms of customer satisfaction? Value Stream Mapping (VSM) and systems thinking may provide means to realize and make such effects visible.

## Agility For Successful AI Adoption

For many current software development companies and organizations, new AI tools are technological changes coming from external environment. Agility is needed to sense and respond to such changes effectively and appropriately. As AI technology is currently changing fast and occasionally even radically, timely responses at the enterprise level may be necessary just to remain competitive. All that calls for business, organizational and enterprise agility capabilities.

To begin with, agility requires continuous scanning and sensing of potential changes in the business and technological environments, possibly even anticipating them. In case of AI technology, the rapid development of the different AI tools, such as LLMs, creates pressure for organizations to stay continuously aware of what is currently available and possibly coming up soon.

Then, responding to the external changes effectively and in a timely way may require many kinds of actions in the organization. For instance, if AI-assistants for coding enable software developers to produce software faster, an agile software organization with strong organizational agility may decide to reallocate the developers in entirely new ways across the organization. With business agility, it may recognize that such reallocations could enable developing additional new products. Enterprise agility helps to ensure that these moves are managed across the organization and aligned with the overall strategic directions.

## Conclusion

Effective AI adoption in software organizations is not simply a matter of introducing new AI technology tools. It incorporates changes in how work is done, where value is created, where bottlenecks emerge, and how organizations need to sense and respond to technological and business change. Agility is required at all levels.

Therefore, successful AI adoption should not be treated just as a technology rollout. It is fundamentally an enterprise-level learning and adaptation challenge. The organizations that benefit most from AI will be those that can understand its effects across the whole system of software work and adapt their business, organizational, and enterprise capabilities accordingly—with agility.
